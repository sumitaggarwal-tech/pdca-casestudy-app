Case Study Overview

Company Profile

Company: Starbucks Corporation

Industry: Service Sector - Quick Service Restaurant / Global Coffeehouse Chain

Scope: Over 30,000 stores worldwide

Starbucks is the world's largest coffeehouse chain, known for premium coffee and customer experience. As a service-sector leader, operational efficiency and customer satisfaction are critical to business success.

Business Challenge

Problem Statement: Long customer wait times were negatively impacting customer satisfaction and business performance.

Why PDCA Methodology?

Starbucks chose the PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) methodology because:

  • Systematic Approach: PDCA provides a structured framework for problem-solving rather than reactive fixes
  • Data-Driven: Emphasizes measurement and analysis before and after changes
  • Risk Mitigation: The pilot testing approach (DO phase) reduces risk of company-wide failures
  • Continuous Improvement: PDCA is a cycle that enables ongoing refinement and learning
  • Standardization: Ensures successful solutions are documented and replicated across all locations

PLAN Phase: Problem Analysis & Root Cause Identification

Step 1: Problem Identification

The PLAN phase began with clearly defining the problems:

  • Long customer wait times averaging 6.0 minutes
  • Peak period wait times reaching 13.3 minutes
  • Only 50% of orders completed within 4 minutes
  • Order sequencing inefficiencies between mobile, drive-thru, and in-store channels
  • Inadequate staffing during peak hours
  • Complex menu causing preparation delays

Step 2: Data Collection Methods

Comprehensive data was gathered through:

  • Time Tracking: Customer wait time tracking over 3 months at multiple locations
  • Channel Analysis: Order completion time analysis by channel (in-store, mobile, drive-thru)
  • Pattern Identification: Peak hour traffic pattern analysis
  • Customer Feedback: Customer satisfaction surveys and complaint analysis
  • Process Observation: Service process observation and time-motion studies
  • Workload Assessment: Barista workload and capacity analysis

Step 3: Quality Tools for Root Cause Analysis

1. Fishbone Diagram (Ishikawa Diagram)

‚ñº

Purpose: Systematically identify ALL potential root causes across the 6M categories

Categories Analyzed:

  • Method (Process):
    • Order sequencing follows first-come-first-served
    • No prioritization between channels
    • Inefficient drink preparation routines
  • Machine (Equipment):
    • Equipment capacity constraints
    • Outdated order management system
    • Lack of automated sequencing technology
  • Manpower (People):
    • Insufficient peak staffing levels
    • Training gaps in efficiency techniques
    • High employee turnover
  • Material (Inputs):
    • Overly complex menu with too many options
    • High degree of drink customizations
  • Measurement:
    • Lack of real-time wait time tracking
    • No standardized service time goals (this was the first time in company history setting a 4-minute goal!)
  • Environment:
    • Variable peak traffic patterns
    • Physical layout constraints at some locations

Key Insight: 15 distinct potential causes identified across all 6M categories, providing comprehensive view of the problem

2. 5 Whys Analysis

‚ñº

Purpose: Drill down from surface symptoms to the fundamental root cause

Problem: Why are customers waiting more than 4 minutes?

‚Üì Why 1: Because orders take too long to prepare

‚Üì Why 2: Because baristas are handling multiple order types simultaneously without prioritization

‚Üì Why 3: Because there is no intelligent system to sequence orders efficiently

‚Üì Why 4: Because the first-come-first-served approach doesn't account for order complexity or channel differences

‚Üì Why 5: Because the company lacks technology-enabled order management and intelligent sequencing

ROOT CAUSE: Absence of intelligent order sequencing algorithm and optimized workflow system

Key Insight: The fundamental issue wasn't just "slow service" but the lack of an intelligent system to manage order flow efficiently

Step 4: Targets Established

Based on analysis, specific, measurable targets were set:

Primary Target:

Reduce average wait time from 6.0 to 4.0 minutes

Secondary Targets:

  • 75% of orders completed under 4 minutes during peak hours (up from 50%)
  • Reduce peak wait time from 13.3 to under 5.5 minutes
  • Maintain mobile order efficiency without delays
  • Improve customer satisfaction scores

Important Note: This was the FIRST TIME in Starbucks history that the company established specific service time goals!

Step 5: Action Plan Developed

Based on root cause analysis and prioritization, the following action plan was created:

  • Primary Solution: Develop and implement intelligent order sequencing algorithm
  • Technology Partner: Partner with Empower Delivery for algorithm development
  • Pilot Program: Select 700 stores for initial pilot testing
  • Training: Create comprehensive barista training programs for new workflows
  • Menu Simplification: Reduce menu complexity by 30%
  • Equipment Upgrade: Implement Siren Craft System technology at high-volume stores
  • Monitoring: Establish real-time monitoring and reporting system
  • Staffing: Adjust peak hour staffing models

DO Phase: Solution Implementation & Pilot Testing

Implementation Strategy

The DO phase involved executing the action plan developed in the PLAN phase, with a focus on controlled pilot testing before company-wide rollout.

Why Pilot Testing? Rather than implementing changes across all 30,000+ stores immediately, Starbucks used a pilot approach to:

  • Test solutions in real-world conditions
  • Identify issues before full-scale deployment
  • Gather data to validate effectiveness
  • Refine the approach based on learnings
  • Reduce risk of company-wide failure

Implementation Activities

Technology Implementation: Order Sequencing Algorithm

‚ñº

Description: Rules-based intelligent algorithm that sequences orders from multiple channels

Functionality:

  • Prioritizes orders based on complexity and preparation time
  • Balances in-store, mobile, and drive-thru orders intelligently
  • Prevents mobile orders from sitting too long (customer pickup experience)
  • Optimizes barista workflow to minimize idle time
  • Dynamically adjusts based on real-time store conditions

Development:

Developed by a team of 6 engineers in partnership with Empower Delivery, a technology firm specializing in order management systems for quick-service restaurants.

Deployment:

Installed at 700 pilot stores with real-time monitoring capabilities

Impact: This technology addressed the #1 root cause (order sequencing inefficiency = 35% of delays)

Equipment Upgrade: Siren Craft System

‚ñº

Description: Advanced beverage-making equipment designed to reduce drink preparation complexity and time

Features:

  • Streamlined beverage creation process
  • Improved equipment ergonomics for faster barista movements
  • Faster milk steaming and espresso extraction
  • Reduced steps for complex drinks

Deployment Strategy:

Initially deployed in highest-volume pilot stores where equipment capacity was a limiting factor

Impact: Addressed equipment capacity limitations (12% of delays) and reduced preparation time for complex drinks (20% of delays)

Training Programs

‚ñº

Comprehensive training was critical for successful implementation:

Training Modules:

  • New Beverage Routines: Simplified, standardized preparation methods to reduce variation and improve speed
  • Algorithm Usage: How to work with the order prioritization system and understand sequencing logic
  • Efficiency Techniques: Time-saving best practices and optimal movement patterns
  • Customer Communication: Managing customer expectations during transition period
  • Siren System Operation: For stores receiving equipment upgrades

Training Delivery:

All baristas at 700 pilot stores received hands-on training before algorithm deployment. Ongoing coaching provided by shift supervisors.

Key Learning: Barista buy-in was critical. Initial resistance was overcome through clear communication about how the system would make their jobs easier during peak periods.

Menu Simplification

‚ñº

Action: Reduced menu complexity by eliminating 30% of menu items

Approach:

  • Analyzed sales data to identify low-volume, high-complexity items
  • Removed seasonal items that required special ingredients
  • Streamlined customization options
  • Focused menu on core, popular offerings
Impact: Directly addressed complex menu items cause (20% of delays) while improving inventory management

Process Changes

‚ñº

Key Workflow Changes:

  • Order Sequencing: Moved from first-come-first-served to algorithm-driven intelligent sequencing
  • Station Separation: Separated ordering and preparation stations where space permitted
  • Timing Goals: Implemented specific 4-minute timing goals (first time in company history!)
  • Visual Management: Created order tracking boards showing queue status
  • Feedback Loops: Established daily huddles for baristas to share insights with management
  • Standard Operating Procedures: Documented new workflows in updated SOPs

CHECK Phase: Results Measurement & Analysis

Measurement Methods

The CHECK phase involved rigorous data collection and analysis to validate whether the solutions implemented in the DO phase achieved the targets set in the PLAN phase.

Data Collection Approach:

  • Real-Time Tracking: Order tracking through the new technology platform providing continuous data
  • Automated Calculation: Wait time automatically calculated and reported for every order
  • Customer Feedback: Surveys at pilot locations to measure satisfaction changes
  • Quality Monitoring: Order accuracy tracked to ensure quality wasn't sacrificed for speed
  • Staff Metrics: Employee performance and satisfaction measured
  • Comparative Analysis: Before-and-after comparison across all metrics

Analysis Tools Used in CHECK Phase

Control Charts & Statistical Process Control (SPC)

‚ñº

Purpose: Monitor wait time variation over time and detect whether improvements were consistent and sustained

What They Measured:

  • Average wait time trends over the pilot period
  • Variation in wait times (consistency)
  • Whether the process was "in control" (predictable)
  • Detection of any unusual patterns or outliers
Key Insight: Control charts showed consistent improvement after algorithm implementation. The process became more stable and predictable, not just faster on average.

Performance Dashboards

‚ñº

Purpose: Provide real-time visibility into key performance indicators across all pilot stores

Dashboard Components:

  • Average wait time by store, district, and company-wide
  • Percentage of orders meeting 4-minute target
  • Peak hour performance metrics
  • Channel performance (in-store vs mobile vs drive-thru)
  • Order accuracy rates
  • Customer satisfaction scores

Store managers and district leaders could monitor performance daily and identify stores needing additional support.

Gaps & Areas for Continued Improvement

The CHECK phase also identified areas that still needed attention:

  • Capacity Constraints: Some very high-volume stores still faced capacity constraints that process efficiency alone couldn't solve (need equipment/space expansion)
  • Mobile Order Timing: Mobile order pickup time prediction needed refinement for better customer experience
  • Ongoing Training: Continuous staff training required to maintain performance as new baristas are hired
  • Equipment Needs: Not all locations received Siren System upgrades; those without it showed less improvement
  • Algorithm Refinement: Opportunities to further optimize sequencing rules based on learnings
Important: Identifying gaps in the CHECK phase is critical because it informs the next PDCA cycle and ensures continuous improvement rather than declaring victory prematurely.

ACT Phase: Standardization & Continuous Improvement

Why the ACT Phase Matters

The ACT phase is where successful pilots become permanent improvements and learnings are captured. Without this phase, improvements might fade over time or fail to spread beyond pilot locations.

Two Key Activities in ACT Phase:

  1. Standardize what worked (make it the new normal)
  2. Plan the next PDCA cycle to address remaining gaps and continue improving

Standardization Actions

Based on the successful pilot results validated in CHECK phase, Starbucks took the following standardization actions:

Company-Wide Rollout

‚ñº
  • Order sequencing algorithm deployed to ALL qualifying stores nationwide
  • Rollout expanded beyond the initial 700 pilot stores to hundreds more
  • Implementation plan created for remaining stores over following quarters
  • Technology infrastructure scaled to support company-wide deployment

Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) Updated

‚ñº
  • All process changes documented in updated Standard Operating Procedures
  • New beverage preparation routines formalized
  • Order sequencing workflow standardized across all stores
  • Visual management standards documented
  • SOPs distributed to all stores and made part of new employee onboarding

Company-Wide Service Goal Established

‚ñº
Historic Milestone: For the first time in company history, Starbucks established a specific service time goal of 4 minutes as a company-wide standard!

This goal became part of:

  • Store performance metrics
  • Manager accountability measures
  • Training materials
  • Daily operations expectations

Training Curriculum Formalized

‚ñº
  • Barista training curriculum updated to include new workflows
  • Algorithm system training made mandatory for all new hires
  • Efficiency techniques incorporated into standard training
  • Ongoing refresher training scheduled quarterly
  • Train-the-trainer program created for shift supervisors

Monitoring & Reporting Systems

‚ñº
  • Real-time performance dashboards made available to all store managers
  • Daily wait time reporting automated
  • Weekly performance reviews at store level
  • Monthly district-level analysis
  • Quarterly executive reviews of company-wide trends
  • Alert system for stores falling below targets

Visual Management Standards

‚ñº
  • Order tracking boards standardized across stores
  • Performance metrics posted in back-of-house areas
  • Target time reminders at beverage stations
  • Visual cues for algorithm-sequenced orders

Continuous Improvement Initiatives

The ACT phase doesn't just standardize‚Äîit also launches the NEXT PDCA cycle. Starbucks identified these continuous improvement opportunities:

Next PDCA Cycle Opportunities:

  • Algorithm Enhancement: Further optimize sequencing algorithm using AI/machine learning based on millions of orders processed
  • Equipment Expansion: Extend Siren Craft System to more locations, prioritizing high-volume stores
  • Mobile Order Timing: Pilot test mobile order pickup time window scheduling feature to improve customer experience
  • Menu Optimization: Explore additional menu simplification opportunities
  • Staff Scheduling: Enhance staff scheduling optimization using predictive analytics
  • Store Layout: Test improved physical layouts at select locations to reduce barista movement

Ongoing Monitoring Plan

To ensure improvements are sustained over time:

Lessons Learned

Key insights captured from the PDCA journey:

  • Holistic Approach Required: Technology + process + people changes needed together for success. Technology alone wouldn't have worked without training and process redesign.
  • Employee Input Critical: Barista feedback during pilot was invaluable for refining the solution and gaining buy-in.
  • Data-Driven Essential: Using quality tools (Fishbone, 5 Whys, Pareto) to identify root causes prevented wasting resources on symptoms rather than causes.
  • Pilot Testing Reduces Risk: Testing at 700 stores before company-wide rollout allowed refinement and proved concept before massive investment.
  • Quality AND Speed Possible: Customer experience improved when service speed increased without sacrificing quality or accuracy.
  • Standardization Sustains Gains: Documenting in SOPs and training ensures improvements don't fade with staff turnover.
  • Continuous Improvement Mindset: Viewing PDCA as a cycle rather than a one-time project creates culture of ongoing enhancement.

Organizational Impact

Business Results:

  • Working to reverse 5-quarter same-store sales decline
  • Improved competitive position in quick-service coffee market
  • Enhanced brand reputation for quality service
  • Increased customer loyalty and repeat visits

Cultural Impact:

  • Created culture of continuous improvement throughout organization
  • Improved employee morale and retention
  • Demonstrated power of data-driven decision making
  • Built capability for future PDCA initiatives
  • Empowered frontline employees to contribute to solutions
